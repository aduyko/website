Wikipedia Doesn’t Actually Need Your Money

This has been covered in depth on a number of other blogs and websites but. I think it’s worth sharing, just in case you feel any pangs of guilt whenever you see Wikipedia post it’s annual “give us some money” banners. Your donation is (mostly) not going towards keeping Wikipedia up and running. If donations suddenly completely stopped, they have enough money to keep the website running for years. The Wikimedia Foundation, who the donations go to, does a lot more than just run Wikipedia. I couldn’t personally tell you what else they do (I don’t think you could tell me either. They do… non-profit… stuff), but they exist and are out there making things happen.

And maybe that’s a cause you find worthy! I think Wikipedia is an incredible, incredible resource. I’m thankful that it exists. I just think their banners are misleading!! I don’t know about you, but seeing them always makes me feel like Wikipedia is constantly under threat of collapsing. That is their trick, intentionally, in order to raise more money, which is their job as a nonprofit organization.

Wikipedia isn’t going anywhere. Their revenue increases year over year. In 2015 they brought in $75 million, and ~20% of the budget went to operations and the associated work. In 2020 they brought in ~$130 million. They also generally keep a year’s worth of revenue tied up in investments. Donations and budget aside, can you imagine if Google, who rely on Wikipedia for all of their in-line data boxes that pop up whenever you search anything, let them disappear? That’s outrageous. That’s an outrageous thought.

Just to reiterate: if you think the Wikimedia Org are a worthy cause, that’s fantastic. Donate to them if you can! I think they’re generally considered to be a pretty good non-profit, as far as organization and expenditures go. But they are not under any kind of threat of disappearing, and I think that’s the impression that a lot of people (myself included) get. You should donate because you think it’s a valuable resource, not because of vague threats that the website will vanish if you don’t.

Music

———————

The Avalanches - We Will Always Love You (Electronic, “Sampledelia”)

This album hops all over the place and has a lot of guest arists, with each track kind of taking on the qualities of the guest artist. There are some really cool tracks but they vary wildly. This makes me think a little of Daft Punk’s Random Access Memories.

* The Garden - Kiss My Super Bowl Ring (Experimental, Punk)

A little all over the place but I like their vibe and I like what they’re doing. The first track starts off feeling very Polvo and then they kick in a very contemporary synthesized drum sound, get a little hardcore, then transition straight into some weird marching band/grindcore/post-hardcore thing on the next track. It has a lot of contemporary hyperpop sensibilities with a lot of mishmashing of genre and distortion and samples. Really interesting to listen to with lots of little nuggets of joy. “AMPM Truck” is an insanely good song that almost feels like it veers into no-wave territory??? So cool.

* Speed Plans - Eeking Out (Hardcore)

This album is really really good. My favorite thing I listened to this week. Close to the roots of hardcore (almost going even further back and sounding like The Clash or something on tracks like “Highway Starr”) but still feels very fresh and fun to listen to, I like this a lot more than other contemporary hardcore acts. It’s also 15 minutes long; the perfect length for a hardcore record.

Such Gold - Deep In A Hole (Punk)

A little noodly, a little post-y, a little of everything, to be honest. A fun listen, although kind of all over the place and inconsistent - it seems like they have more ideas than they know what to do with and they cram a little too much into every song and individual songs aren’t as ironed-out as I think they could be.

Barlow - Barlow (Dreampop, Shoegaze, Experimental)

This was ok, I don’t really love a lot of shoegaze (don’t get mad at me for what I’m about to write), I think the washed-out sounds are kind of hard to stay engaged in and I don’t know any shoegaze songs that I’ve ever had stuck in my head or that have really stayed with me. [1] I think the lo-fi and more garage/punk-y parts of this album work really well, and I’m a huge sucker for lo-fi, but there just isn’t anything here for me to sink my teeth into.

Barlow - In A Strangers Car (Dreampop, Shoegaze)

I think this is a lot better than the S/T, maybe just because the guitars are louder. It also seems like this album has more pop and punk sensibilities than the S/T, and I think those work really well with the washed-out-y-ness of shoegaze. Honestly though on the whole it still has that general shoegaze smog around it, except for the final track, “Time Man,” which I think is absolutely nuts good.

* ORE - Belatedly (Ambient, Brass, Drone)

The band behind this newsletter’s subtitle, from the bandcamp description: “ORE is the drone, doom tuba sound of Sam Underwood.” I like this kind of drone-y minimal ambient stuff. This is an album originally intended for Underwood’s deceased father, and any kind of personal and sensitive subject matter like that, especially when the music itself sounds appropriate for the material, makes me appreciate it all the more. It’s music with meaning. Soulful dirges.

* Slayer - Reign In Blood (Thrash)

That’s right, it’s Slayer. And they’re looking for the meaning of pain. Last week I wrote about the ZEUS! album Opera and mentioned their song “Giorgio Gaslini is our Tom Araya” in particular. Tom Araya is the lead singer of Slayer, and his history with the band is pretty interesting. He’s a licensed respiratory technician! He pretty much single-handedly funded the band in their early days. They drove around everywhere in his van. He’s a practicing catholic. That’s the part I always find kind of wild with all of the satanic imagery and dark lyrics of the band. “Jesus saves,” but actually, it’s for real.

Also I just found out that they officially split up in 2019. This seems kind of unprecedented for one of these old-school thrash bands; they’ve all been around forever. Metallica are still around. Megadeth are still around. Anthrax are still around (Anthrax!!!!). Testament are still around. Exodus are still around. It’s honestly kind of incredible that they have all lasted this long — I listen to a lot of emo, and emo bands all last like, maybe two years before breaking up. But Slayer have now called it quits! The winds of change.
