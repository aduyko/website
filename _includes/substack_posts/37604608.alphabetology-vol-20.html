Pixel Art

When I was a teen I thought I wanted to make video games and got very into pixel art because of that, and I think pixel art has shaped a lot of my sensibilities when it comes to creating art (drawing, painting). It’s an interesting medium for a few reasons. You have the infinite potential of a computer photo editing tool, but you’re working pixel by pixel and meticulously picking colors and placing them down. You can theoretically create “perfect” art (because you’re going pixel by pixel! There are no accidents!).

The main thing that I think has carried over from pixel art to physical media for me has been my understanding of color. Because a lot of pixel art is created with limited palettes and color limits, pixel artists have picked up various techniques over time to figure out how to make colors that shouldn’t work together work. The below piece by [Helm] uses three colors (and black):

You can zoom in and check if you don’t believe me. But this is what pixel art is all about: working with constraints, whether they be scale or color or whatever else. And it’s impossible to get anywhere if you can’t figure out how to move from color to color. The following two are C64 pieces, which are, aside from color limitations, also limited in how pixels are laid out. The original Commodore 64 could only show two different colors from its wacky palette per 8x8 square. If you look carefully, you can see where the two pieces have borders between these 8x8 squares.

These are by [ptoing] and [electric] respectively. I think it’s kind of tough to appreciate how difficult it is to make pieces like the above within the provided constraints. For context, a lot of C64 art for video games from back when the C64 was a thing (1980s) looked like this:

Another look at the work people do with limited palettes is this portrait exploration by [jeremy], each portrait done using a different limited palette:

I don’t know, maybe it’s just me. But I really feel like the color choices and transitions between colors in pixel art are so cool, and such great inspiration. Actually, you can also take a look at jeremy’s first attempt at a C64 piece to see what I mean about the blockiness that’s hard to avoid:

It looks great! But the squares where the two colors per 8x8 block constraint kick in are really clear.

There’s also slightly less constrained pixel art that people make all the time, and there are arguments about what pixel art is, exactly. I’m not sure there’s any one end-all be-all definition. You know it when you see it. [Snake], the artist who went on to create the art for the game Owlboy, created these two pieces in the mid 2000s:

And [fool] has been a mainstay in the pixel art scene, putting out massive pieces like these:

It’s really hard to overstate how much work goes into these. The sketch and initial work might not be pixel-perfect, but all of the editing and refining is. Pixel art takes a lot of work and talent! And I think it has a lot to teach any artist. The biggest lesson being, if you don’t know how to get from one color to another, slam some gray down in between them and you’re good to go. Or don’t, and it’ll probably still look fine.

Coffee

Coffee is
a most wonderful brew
I’ll have one right now
and again in a few
the dark bitter taste
keeps me up on my toes
as I do all my work
and I sweat through my clothes
and I finish my duties
and look at the clock
it’s a quarter to six
why not a-nother pot
and I’ll feel energized
until I go to bed
at which point I’ll lament
and wish that I were dead
