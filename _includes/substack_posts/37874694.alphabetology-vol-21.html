The apprentice, eager to prove themselves, deliberated for hours before picking a book off of a shelf and bringing it back to the librarian. Ceramics

I love vases and vessels. Sometimes, they are just… capital G Good shapes. Ceramics seem way more primal and magnetic to me than paintings, like they are reaching at some core aesthetic truth. I like them more the less usable they are, too. A good ceramic work is functional in the same way a Churchill martini is a vermouth cocktail. [1]

Lena Medvedeva (who only has an instagram presence! An incredible shame) is a ceramic artist working out of St. Petersburg whose tagline, “sensual minimalism,” applies to the full extent that a vase can be sensual. They are! Look at them! It’s impossible for me to express how important these are to me. They are Good Shapes. They are Great shapes. Looking at them will make you feel something in your heart and you will instantly know what they would feel like if you held them in your hands.

I mean, I don’t have anything more to say. I don’t know enough about pottery. But in my eyes, this is what it’s all about. Funky shapes, sometimes with holes in them.

Look at these cups!!!! LOOK AT THESE CUPS!!!!!! Incredible.

People HATE These Two Weird Translators

Check out this sequence of really long sentences: A friend had an Instagram story with an excerpt from a Lydia Davis essay where she comments on a person who wrote a criticism of her translations of Proust. She and Nabakov are the only two people who easily come to my mind who are both accomplished authors in their own right and who have also written notable translations. Maybe it’s because of this that their own commentaries on translation have crossed my radar moreso than anyone elses. Or maybe it’s because they’re already well known authors, so more people pay attention to the fact that they’re also creating translations.

Anyway, this Instagram story reminded me of my favorite criticism of translations, which is that a lot of people really really really hate the Pevear and Volokhovsky translations of classic Russian literature. There are multiple essays published online about them, I like this one: [link], but here’s the gist:

Volokhovsky does a first pass, doing a literal word for word translation from Russian to English. Then Pevear, who does not speak Russian, goes through and tidies the language up a bit to make it sound more natural in English. They then ping pong and review the result a bunch until they’re happy with it. As a result, you get a bunch of phrases that sound incredibly clunky in English, but, if you speak Russian, it’s pretty easy to translate back and figure out what the original Russian phrase was. And the real tragedy of this is that it leads people reading these translations to believe that incredibly bright and funny authors, like, for example, Gogol, are droll and unpleasant to read.

I think the reason this is at all controversial in any way is that people who don’t like these translations are offended at the number of awards and overwhelming praise that these translations get. Newspaper people love these translations. They are Hot and Exciting.

Translating in general seems like a very difficult business where a large amount of people are going to hate you no matter what. Languages have untranslatable nuances. You wind up with the two big schools of thought around translating that we have now: One group believes that text should be translated as literally as possible and preserve as much of the original text as possible, and one group that believes that translated text should be updated to be readable and modern while maintaining as much of the author’s original intent and spirit as possible. You wind up with things like these P&V translations on one side and things like No Fear Shakespeare on the other.

I can’t imagine working on translating a novel for any reason other than love for the text, and I can’t imagine somehow avoiding riling people up due to this above split in how people understand the purpose of translation. Seems like a big lose-lose-lose-lose situation. Except for for the readers? Maybe? If the translations don’t make them hate the work. What a nightmare.
