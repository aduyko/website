And now for a brief discussion about this Magritte painting, and some apprehensions I have with surrealism in general:

The Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus was published in 1921, years before this was painted, and Wittgenstein writes (3.221 if you want to follow along at home):

Objects can only be named. Signs are their representatives. I can only speak about them: I cannot put them into words. Propositions can only say how things are, not what they are.

Which, 8 years earlier, not only encompasses anything Magritte tried to do with his pipe but goes even further to say that about all propositions, not just pictorial ones.

Plato wrote about forms and separated the idea of a thing from an individual thing back in the BCs. This is all to say, this isn’t a very nuanced or deep painting, and others had already done way more interesting work going way further than this basic idea. There’s nothing about this painting that needs to be a painting.

And that’s kind of my issue with some chunk of surrealist work. It seems very self-involved, and seems more interested in the conversation about our relationship with art than in actually creating good art. I think the purpose of art is communication, and I think great art communicates something that is simultaneously human enough to resonate with others but that is also impossible to express in any other form. I think surrealism, on the other hand, is about the artist showing you a cool idea they had. I think a lot of the dream work of Dali and Magritte really suffers from the artists themselves being unclear on what they’re expressing outside of “these pictures came to me in a dream and I think they’re neat.” Like this Magritte painting:

His statement about it is basically “I woke up from a dream and thought I saw an egg in a birdcage instead of a bird. So I thought, wow, isn’t that crazy, these two things are related, unlike my usual surrealist work, where things aren’t related.” How do you respect something like that? I can’t. I dunno. And maybe that’s the intent: I want to make sense of it, where there is intentionally little sense to make.

With a lot of the work, because there’s so much going on and so little clear message, we wind up psychoanalyzing the artist instead. What could have been going on in their mind that led to this dream/art? And that seems way too self-obsessed for artwork to be for my taste, and kind of explains how pop art came out of this movement. Surrealism seems like it sewed the seeds as a deconstructionist movement of how we relate to art and think about art more than as a movement of what art can do to us, and I conceptually just don’t really like that.

But maybe the surrealists are right and I’m just totally missing the point.
