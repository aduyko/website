I read Atonement by Ian McEwan and watched The Master this week but need some more time to think about them before writing about them. Some quick thoughts though:

I like Atonement a lot as a meta-novel and had a lot of fun reading it, I think it’s really well written. The main couple of things I kind of want to work through for myself before writing about are that everything is very one-dimensional, and the depth of the novel all comes meta-textually, and that the horrific crime that needs atonement is NOT in fact the horrific crime that actually occurs. The actual crime is secondary to the purpose of the novel. It’s a really interesting book. I spoiled it for myself by skipping ahead after getting ~100 pages in and I kind of liked reading it knowing what would happen later.

The Master was a really beautiful movie with an excellent opening sequence that I was not expecting at all. I think it’s maybe the tightest movie that I’ve seen like this that revels in ambiguity. Very little is explained (and very little CAN be explained) but the way the characters grow through the glimpses of them that we see is super consistent and the ending really worked for me. I need some more time to write about this one because of just how open-ended so much of it is. I have a general idea but I feel that it wouldn’t be fair to ignore a lot of the clearer themes and motifs laid out.

Hunks of Metal and Big Rocks

I was upstate for most of this past week and went to see the sculptures at Storm King Art Center and also went to the Dia:Beacon. I’ve been to the Dia:Beacon before but Storm King was new to me. My favorite works of art are giant hunks of rock or metal, and I think these are both excellent art venues that you should visit if you get the chance. I’ll show some of my favorite works from Storm King this week and the Dia:Beacon next week.

This exhibit by Martha Tuttle was really really good. The big rocks were good rocks, and the little rocks all seemed very meticulously decided on and placed to me. Without looking into it further, I imagine the artist took a really long time arranging each individual piece. The statement was also very beautiful — it’s really hard to come by an artist’s statement that comes off as so sincere and so thoughtful without seeming pretentious. It read more like poetry than like a statement.

I liked this piece a lot because I think it’s really difficult to do any kind of work like this where the contents of the painting are really easy to understand but it doesn’t feel like it’s trying to pound you on the head with a clear message. It’s also really funny! It’s fun to just look at but it’s also called “Waiting for UFO.” It makes you smile and it makes you think. Incredible.

This piece is really good and a lot better in person than in the photo because you kind of walk through a little train through a bunch of tall grass and the piece seems almost alien when you encounter it. I feel like repetition is under-explored in gigantic fabricated pieces, and actually maybe part of what I like about Wavefield (above) as well.

Storm King is also just really pleasant to walk around, it’s not so much walking as to be intimidating, and the artworks are spaced apart really well and give you a lot of time to really take them in. It’s a wonderful space.

Music

———————

Paramore - Riot (Rock/Emo Pop)

This was okay. Very fun a lot of the time, a very Panic! at the Disco type emo. You can kind of see how they wound up going the same way as Panic! and turning into more poppy rock with their self titled further down the line. Some of this feels very post-alt-rock, like “When It Rains” or “We Are Broken,” and that leaves a bad taste in my mouth. “That’s What You Get” is a karaoke all-timer but I can’t say there’s anything here I really want to listen to again. It’s not bad though!!!! If I had listened to this in high school I’m sure I would listen to this album at least once a year.

* The Jazz June - The Medicine (Emo/Post-Hardcore)

Very sick, gotta love that album cover. My favorite tracks were “Motorhead’s Roadie” and “Death from Above.” I like this band the most when they get very post-y and almost math-y, like on “Death from Above” where the guitars and drums all seem to be doing their own thing throughout the song. Think emotive hardcore but with lots of dueling guitar lines constantly challenging each other. Really cool band.

Jawbreaker - Unfun (Punk)

I didn’t like this that much. Punk albums have no business being an hour long, and to be honest, this doesn’t seem that much more interesting than a lot of other punk music from around this time. I think this album is at its best when Jawbreaker really push themselves, like on the final track, “Eye-5.”

I actually started listening to their album Dear You, was really enjoying it, but read that that album sounds very different from their prior releases so I went back to this. I do want to go through their discography, for some reason they feel like they would be a really important band for me (in particular) to listen to.
