Monet’s Cathedrals

Everyone loves Monet!!!! He’s super palatable and approachable and still created immensely beautiful work and helped revolutionize art. A powerhouse of an artist. The thing that Monet’s paintings radiate out to me, particularly his series where he would paint the same thing multiple times, such as his cathedrals, is how clear it is that he loved light. He adored it. He was an incredibly honest painter and all of his thoughts come through immediately in his work. My favorite of his series are his paintings of Rouen Cathedral, of which this is a small selection:

I want to cry looking at these. There are 17 paintings he made like this. They are realer than real. There is an overflow of powerful emotion in them. They speak to me. Scroll back through them and think about how vividly you can imagine being in the exact time and place Monet was when he painted them. You can feel the weather; exactly how hot the sun is, whether or not there is a a breeze. You can hear that it’s early in the morning, or maybe that it’s starting to get late. Days pass with you, with Monet, as you gaze upon this majestic cathedral.

The Lobster (2015)

I think the “Man Eats Alone” skit from this movie is one of the funniest things I’ve ever seen in my life, and the follow up “Man Eats With Woman” is equally good. I also really liked that everybody in this movie speaks like a robot and how all of the dialogue consists of people either, apropos of nothing, stating random facts from Wikipedia, or stone cold lying to each other. Amidst all this, you have two beautiful stories about a society that punishes you if you can’t find partnership, and a competing society that punishes you if you can. I loved this movie.

Maybe a spoiler, so watch out: I really like when movies end without closure, so the ending really did it for me too. We spend all this time with Colin Farrell’s character, David, and we still don’t really know anything about him — this is what that final scene reveals to us. He is a flip-floppy man who, more than anything, wants to find a partner. He lives in a world of falsehood, artificiality, and self-preservation. Who is David, really? It’s kind of jarring when you realize the first thing you learn about him is that he’s an architect, in an offhand comment halfway through the movie. What does he really want? What WOULD he do?

But it’s probably best not to linger too much on this thought — the movie is, after all, showing us a farce. A comedy. Surely, real people are not like this? Surely.
